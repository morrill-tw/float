The Product

Float has several features that distinguish it from a typical cup:

  • Its bottom is rounded, making it impossible to set down when holding a drink.
  • It features two sensors on opposite sides of the cup. When one sensor is covered, the cup will vibrate.
  • It lacks any sort of handle.

The Story

In the United States, life is often fast-paced and centered around work. It's rare to find coffee shops and cafes that enforce no laptop policies, thus people often work in these spaces that should really be used for socialization and mindfulness.

That's where Float comes in. Float totally revolutionizes the way that we utilize cafe spaces and transforms our everyday routines. Float's design features essentially prevent customers from doing anything but hold on to the cup with both hands by causing a vibrating sensation when held by only one hand, and toppling over when settled down. This eliminates phone and laptop usage. It encourages customers to be present, engage in more conversation, or spend time with their own thoughts. This fosters a healthier and more vibrant cafe experience.

Float can also be used in the home, encouraging mindfulness, and a break in one's daily routine. With the advent of social media platforms and new technologies like artificial intelligence, we spend much of our time glued to our phones, distracted, and in the zone. The average American spends about 7 hours a day looking at screens. 7 hours! It's important to spend time reflecting on our days and our wellbeing, not drowning out our thoughts with endless scrolling. Float forces this kind of thinking.

There's a greater conversation to be had here. Why do we feel inclined to work in environments that weren't traditionally intended for that purpose? Why do we struggle to give ourselves breaks? Why do we avoid sitting with nothing but our own thoughts? Is that healthy? These are all important questions to ponder.

Design Process

Float went through many iterations. The beginning stages of the design process regarded understanding my audience, understanding my purpose, and getting the shape of Float right. Whenever I walk into a cafe, I can't help but feel frustrated with those on their laptops or phones. So my target audience became cafe customers. I recognize that many customers use cafe spaces for this purpose, doing work, so I set out to change that. The first few sketches pictured below show some of my first iterations of the product. I knew that I wanted to force cafe customers to hold this cup in a way that would prevent them from using their hands. I then dove into some of the more technical aspects of the cup, like charging, and how to minimize heat transfer, but I found this to be distracting from my main goal, to shape cafe customer behavior. So I continued on, and eventually got to a point where I started sketching logos, and drafts for this web page. The name Float comes from the fact that the cup cannot be placed down and alludes to the floating sensation that one may feel when meditating. Finally, I created some neater representations of the product, and its use, which made their way to this page.
